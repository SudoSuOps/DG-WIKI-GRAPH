# DG Site Selection — What Makes a Dollar General Location

**Source**: DG 10-K (FY2026), investor presentations, USDA food desert data, Census Bureau, DG real estate disclosures
**Relevance**: Understanding site selection tells you WHERE new DGs will appear, WHY existing stores are defensible, and WHICH locations carry renewal risk. This is underwriting intelligence.

## The Rural Fortress Strategy

Dollar General's competitive moat is simple: go where Walmart won't.

```
MARKET POSITION:
  75-80% of DG stores are in towns under 20,000 population
  60% of DG customers earn $30,000/year or less
  Average DG customer drives 3-5 miles to reach the store
  
  DG REACHES PROFITABILITY IN MARKETS UNDER 10,000 POPULATION
  
  Walmart needs 50,000+ population to justify a Supercenter
  Dollar Tree needs 15,000+ population for most formats
  DG operates profitably at 5,000-8,000 population
  
  THIS IS THE MOAT:
  In a town of 7,000 people, DG may be the ONLY national retailer.
  No competition. No alternative. Captive market.
  That's why small-town DG leases are fortress assets.
```

## Site Selection Criteria — What DG Looks For

### Physical Requirements

```
SITE SPEC:
  Lot size:               1.0 acre minimum (1.0 - 1.5 typical)
  Frontage:               100+ feet on primary road
  Visibility:             High — must be visible from 500+ feet
  Ingress/egress:         FULL — right-in/right-out minimum, full turns preferred
  Speed limit:            35-55 mph preferred (high traffic, but not highway speed)
  Traffic count (ADT):    5,000 - 25,000 vehicles/day
  Curb cuts:              Dedicated, not shared with adjacent parcels
  Signage:                Pylon sign rights required (30+ ft height typical)
  Grade:                  Level to slight grade — no significant slopes
  Flood zone:             Zone X preferred (minimal flood hazard)
  Environmental:          Phase I clean — no contamination history
  Utilities:              Water, sewer (septic acceptable), electric, telecom
```

### Location Criteria

```
PREFERRED CO-TENANCY:
  ✓ Schools (K-12)           — daily parent traffic
  ✓ Post offices             — regular foot traffic
  ✓ Churches                 — community anchor
  ✓ Gas stations             — trip consolidation
  ✓ Community centers        — civic gathering points
  ✓ Medical clinics          — regular patient visits
  ✓ Small grocery / IGA      — complementary, not competitive
  
AVOID:
  ✗ Industrial zones         — wrong customer profile
  ✗ Highway interchanges     — too fast, no walkability
  ✗ Flood-prone lowlands     — insurance, access risk
  ✗ Environmentally sensitive — permitting delays, liability
  ✗ Limited access roads     — poor ingress/egress
```

## DG's Proprietary Algorithm

DG uses sophisticated location analytics that go beyond basic demographics:

```
KNOWN ALGORITHM FACTORS:
  1. Traffic patterns       — time-of-day flow, directional splits
  2. Speed limits           — 35-55 mph sweet spot for turn-in behavior
  3. Road classification    — state highway > county road > local street
  4. Competitive proximity  — distance to Walmart, Dollar Tree, Family Dollar
  5. Income stratification  — block-group level, not ZIP code
  6. Commute patterns       — DG intercepts drive-to-work routes
  7. Store-to-store spacing — minimum distance between DG locations
  8. Seasonal population    — tourist areas, college towns adjust
  9. Growth trajectory      — new housing permits, population trend
  10. Market defensibility  — can a competitor viably enter this market?
```

## The Quality Shift — FY2026 Signal

```
FY2026 STORE ACTIONS:
  New stores opened:       ~450
  Stores closed:           96 (underperforming traditional DG)
  pOpshelf closures:       45
  Remodels:                ~2,000 (mature stores)
  DGTP projects:           ~2,250 (transformation program)
  Relocations:             47
  
  WHAT THIS MEANS:
  DG is shifting from "growth at all costs" to QUALITY OVER QUANTITY.
  
  96 store closures = 96 dark buildings = 96 landlords with vacancy risk.
  This is REAL risk for DG net lease owners.
  
  BUT: 450 new stores + 4,250 remodels = DG is investing in WINNERS.
  A remodeled store is a store DG plans to keep for 10+ more years.
  
  BROKER INTELLIGENCE:
  Check if the DG you're pricing has been remodeled recently.
  Recently remodeled = DG committed to the location = strong renewal probability.
  No remodel + older building + declining area = elevated risk.
```

## Cannibalization Strategy

```
DG WILL CANNIBALIZE ITS OWN STORES TO DEFEND MARKET SHARE.

  If a competitor (Dollar Tree, Family Dollar) threatens a market,
  DG will open a second store in the same trade area — even if it
  reduces sales at the first store.
  
  WHY: Two DGs at 75% each deny the market to a competitor entirely.
  One DG at 100% invites a Dollar Tree next door.
  
  WHAT THIS MEANS FOR STNL:
  - Two DGs in the same small town is NOT necessarily a red flag
  - DG made a deliberate defensive decision
  - BOTH leases carry the same BBB corporate guarantee
  - But: cannibalized stores may have lower per-store revenue
  - Lower revenue = higher risk of non-renewal at option
  
  CHECK: If you see two DGs within 3 miles in a town under 15K,
  check when each opened. The newer one is likely the defensive play.
```

## Dark Store Risk — What Kills a Location

```
DG CLOSES STORES WHEN:
  1. Population decline         — rural depopulation, factory closure
  2. Road rerouting             — bypass built, traffic diverts
  3. Competitive saturation     — new Walmart Neighborhood Market opens
  4. Lease economics            — rent above market, DG relocates nearby
  5. Building condition         — landlord won't maintain, DG won't renew
  6. Crime / safety             — repeated incidents, employee safety
  7. Natural disaster           — flood damage, not worth rebuilding
  8. Cannibalization backfire   — defensive store underperforms both locations
  
  96 CLOSURES IN FY26 OUT OF 20,959 STORES = 0.46% CLOSURE RATE
  That's low. But if YOUR listing is one of the 96, it's 100% for you.
```

## Site Selection as Underwriting Intelligence

When you evaluate a DG property, reverse-engineer the site selection:

| Factor | Strong Location | Weak Location |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Population trend | Growing or stable | Declining |
| Competition | DG is only national retailer | Walmart within 10 miles |
| Traffic count | 10,000+ ADT | Under 5,000 ADT |
| Road type | State highway, main corridor | Side street, limited access |
| Visibility | Prominent corner, pylon sign | Set back, obstructed |
| Recent remodel | Yes (DG investing) | No (DG may be evaluating) |
| Co-tenancy | School, post office, church | Isolated, no anchors |
| Store age | Under 10 years | Over 15 years (approaching term) |
| Market income | $25K-$50K median HHI | Under $20K (extremely low) |
| Nearest DG | 5+ miles | Under 3 miles (cannibalization) |

```
SCORING A LOCATION (broker shorthand):
  8-10 strong factors = FORTRESS — price at market cap rate
  5-7 strong factors  = SOLID — price at market, note any risks
  3-4 strong factors  = CAUTION — widen cap rate 25-50 bps
  0-2 strong factors  = RISK — disclose heavily, price accordingly
```

## Broker Takeaway

DG's site selection is your underwriting cheat sheet. If DG chose the location, they ran the algorithm. If they remodeled it, they're committed. If they closed it, they did the math and walked. Every DG property tells a story — your job is to read it and price accordingly.
